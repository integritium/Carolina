A B S T R A C T
Traditional neural networks employ fixed weights during inference, limiting their ability to adapt to changing 
input conditions, unlike biological neurons that adjust signal strength dynamically based on stimuli. This 
discrepancy between artificial and biological neurons constrains neural network flexibility and adaptability. 
To bridge this gap, we propose a novel framework for adaptive neural networks, where neuron weights are 
modeled as functions of the input signal, allowing the network to adjust dynamically in real-time. Importantly, 
we achieve this within the same traditional architecture of an Artificial Neural Network, maintaining structural 
familiarity while introducing dynamic adaptability. In our research, we apply Chebyshev polynomials as one 
of the many possible decomposition methods to achieve this adaptive weighting mechanism, with polynomial 
coefficients learned during training. Of the 145 datasets tested, our adaptive Chebyshev neural network 
demonstrated a marked improvement over an equivalent MLP in approximately 83% of the cases, performing 
strictly better on 121 datasets. In the remaining 24 datasets, the performance of our algorithm matched that 
of the MLP, highlighting its ability to generalize the behavior of standard neural networks while offering 
enhanced adaptability. As a generalized form of MLP, this model seamlessly retains MLP performance where 
needed while extending its capabilities to achieve superior accuracy across a wide range of complex tasks. 
These results underscore the potential of adaptive neurons to enhance generalization, flexibility, and robustness 
in neural networks, particularly in applications with dynamic or non-linear data dependencies.
1. Introduction
1.1. Biological basis of neurons and their inspiration for Artificial Neural 
Networks
Human neurons are specialized cells responsible for transmitting 
signals within the brain and nervous system, enabling cognition, sen-
sation, and motor control. The response of neurons to input signals is 
inherently nonlinear. Neurons exhibit threshold behavior, where they 
fire action potentials only when the input surpasses a certain thresh-
old (Koch, 2004). Below this threshold, the neuron remains inactive, 
regardless of slight variations in input. Furthermore, neurons integrate 
signals over time through temporal summation, where multiple inputs 
arriving within a short time window can summate, potentially pushing 
the neuron past its threshold. Spatial summation also occurs, where 
inputs from different locations on the dendrites have varying effects 
on the soma, depending on their proximity and strength (Bliss and 
Collingridge, 1993). Another important factor contributing to nonlin-
earity is synaptic plasticity, where the strength of synaptic connections

Chebyshev polynomials are particularly well suited for this adaptive

input–weight relationships, while their minimax approximation behav-

Additionally, their efficient recursive computation allows higher-order 
polynomial terms to be evaluated with minimal computational over-
head. Together, these characteristics provide a balance of expressive-
ness, numerical stability, and efficiency. The mathematically foun-

Chebyshev-based adaptive neuron offers several advantages (Mason

• Dynamic Weight Adaptation: Each weight adjusts continuously 
to changing inputs, improving flexibility compared to static-
weight neurons.
• Improved Generalization: Orthogonality and strong approxima-
tion capability help prevent overfitting, especially in nonlinear 
tasks.
• Computational Efficiency: Recursive evaluation ensures the

2. Related work
Research on adaptive neural architectures spans several directions, 
including example-dependent computation, dynamic parameterization, 
polynomial-based models, spiking mechanisms, and networks that re-
place static weights with learnable functions. Below we summarize the 
most relevant lines of work and highlight how our approach differs.
2.1. Adaptive computation in neural networks
A prominent class of adaptive models focuses on reducing inference 
cost by adjusting the amount of computation per example. This includes 
early-exit architectures and adaptive subnetwork selection (Bolukbasi 
et al., 2017), where simpler examples are routed through shallow 
modules while more challenging cases trigger deeper evaluation. These 
methods improve efficiency but do not modify weights as a function of 
the input; the underlying parameters remain fixed during inference.
2.2. Dynamic weighting and conditional parameterization
Several approaches introduce dynamically generated parameters 
based on input features, such as attention mechanisms, conditionally 
parameterized convolutions (CondConv) (Yang et al., 2019), dynamic 
convolutions (Chen et al., 2020), and deformable convolutions (Dai 
et al., 2017). These methods combine multiple pre-learned kernels 
or spatial offsets using learned gating or attention scores. Although 
they incorporate input dependence, the generated weights are typically 
derived from small auxiliary networks and do not use an explicit 
functional decomposition of weights. In contrast, our model defines 
each weight directly as a polynomial function of the input, providing a 
principled and interpretable functional form.
2.3. Chebyshev polynomials in neural architectures
Chebyshev polynomials have been used in neural networks for 
feature expansion, spectral graph convolution, and function approx-
imation. Most relevant is the work of Troumbis et al. (2020), who 
construct a multilayer model whose activations incorporate truncated 
Chebyshev series and whose parameters are optimized via Differential 
Evolution. Their formulation uses polynomials to approximate the net-
work output itself rather than the weights of individual neurons, and 
does not provide input-dependent weight dynamics or compatibility 
with gradient-based learning. We formalize these distinctions explicitly 
below.
2.3.1. Differences from Troumbis et al. (2020)
While both our work and Troumbis et al. (2020) utilize Chebyshev 
polynomials within neural architectures, the underlying philosophy, 
design, and learning mechanisms differ significantly:
• Static vs. Adaptive Weighting: Troumbis et al. employ Cheby-
shev polynomials as part of a fixed series expansion to approxi-
mate the network output. In contrast, our method uses Chebyshev 
polynomials to define input-dependent adaptive weights at the 
neuron level. This dynamic formulation allows weights to change 
with varying input stimuli, thereby offering greater biological 
plausibility and flexibility.

• Learning Mechanism: Troumbis et al. use a population-based op-

does not rely on gradient descent. Our model, on the other hand,

• Architectural Scope and Generality: The model in Troumbis 
et al. is tailored to specific regression problems with a fixed 
architectural structure. Our model generalizes this by integrating 
polynomial-based adaptive weights directly into the neuron for-

decompositions beyond Chebyshev.
• Biological Motivation and Neural Interpretation: While
Troumbis et al. focus primarily on mathematical function ap-
proximation, our formulation is bio-inspired—aiming to model 
the dynamic response characteristics of biological neurons where 
synaptic strength adapts in real-time based on the nature of 
incoming signals.
In summary, our approach significantly extends the scope and ap-
plicability of Chebyshev-based neural models by introducing dynamic 
adaptability, backpropagation-based learning, and broader architec-
tural generalization, thereby addressing a different set of scientific and 
practical challenges.
2.4. Spiking Neural Networks and biological adaptation
Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) incorporate temporal coding and 
event-driven communication inspired by biological neurons (Guo et al., 
2023). Training strategies such as ANN-to-SNN conversion (Han and 
Roy, 2020) and surrogate gradient learning (Fang et al., 2021) enable 
SNNs to approximate continuous neural models. Adaptive SNNs fur-
ther introduce mechanisms like spike-frequency adaptation (Zambrano 
et al., 2019). While SNNs emulate biological temporal dynamics, they 
rely on nondifferentiable spike events and discrete communication. 
Our model shares the goal of biological inspiration but instead uses 
continuous, differentiable weight functions, making it directly trainable 
with backpropagation and applicable to standard deep learning tasks.
2.5. Functional weight representations and inspiration from KANs
Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs) (Liu et al., 2024) replace 
fixed scalar weights with learnable univariate functions defined on the 
edges, improving expressivity relative to standard MLPs. This architec-
tural idea, representing weights as functions, motivates our framework. 
Unlike KANs, which use spline-based activations, we employ Chebyshev 
polynomial expansions to parameterize weights as explicit functions 
of the input. This yields smooth, interpretable, and computationally 
efficient adaptive weights while retaining the familiar structure of an 
MLP.
3. Proposed architecture
This section introduces the adaptive neuron model in which each 
weight is replaced by an input-dependent function modeled through 
Chebyshev polynomial expansion. The architectural structure remains 
identical to a standard multilayer perceptron (MLP), ensuring compat-
ibility with existing deep learning workflows while enabling dynamic 
weight modulation.

3.3. Layer integration
The adaptive neuron integrates cleanly into a fully connected layer. 
For an input vector 𝐱∈R𝑛, the Chebyshev-transformed tensor has 
dimension 𝑛× (𝑘+ 1). The learnable coefficients 𝑐𝑖,𝑗 form a correspond-
ing parameter matrix. The final adaptive layer output is obtained by 
evaluating Eq. (4) for all neurons in the layer.
This formulation preserves the expressiveness of MLPs while aug-
menting them with dynamic, input-aware behavior.
3.4. Training and optimization
All parameters 𝑐𝑖,𝑗 are trained directly through standard backprop-
agation. Automatic differentiation computes the required gradients 
using the chain rule applied to Eqs. (3)–(4). The model is optimized 
using Adam, which provides stable convergence for the polynomial 
coefficient updates.
Because the architecture remains fully differentiable and struc-
turally identical to an MLP aside from the weight functions, training 
and scalability are unchanged from standard feedforward networks.

4. Data and experiment
4.1. Datasets
Experiments were conducted on 145 classification datasets from the 
Penn Machine Learning Benchmarks (PMLB) (Olson et al., 2017). These 
datasets span a wide range of sizes (42–105,908 samples), dimensional-
ities (2–1000 features), and label complexities (2–26 classes), providing 
a diverse and rigorous test bed for evaluating model robustness. Fig. 
2 summarizes the distribution of dataset sizes, feature dimensions, and 
target classes. This diversity ensures that the proposed adaptive neuron 
model is evaluated across both low-dimensional and high-dimensional 
tasks, as well as binary and multi-class problems. A more detailed 
description of all datasets used can be found in Appendix D.
4.2. Experiment framework
The experiment compares a standard MLP with the proposed
Chebyshev-enhanced model using identical architectures and training 
procedures. For each dataset:
1. Split data into training and test sets.
2. Train an MLP ten times; record the best test accuracy.
3. Train the Chebyshev model ten times on the same split; record 
the best test accuracy.
Fig. 3 illustrates the workflow of our experiment, showing the 
repeated training and evaluation steps for each model type. This pro-
cedure ensures a fair, variance-reduced comparison.
4.2.1. Model architecture
Both models use the same three-layer topology: 4 neurons in the 
first layer, 2 in the second layer, and an output layer matching the 
number of classes. The Chebyshev model applies a degree-3 Chebyshev 
expansion to the input of each layer, but otherwise matches the MLP 
architecture exactly.
Fig. 3. Experiment workflow for training and evaluating the MLP and Cheby-
shev models.
4.2.2. Training setup
Both models are trained under identical conditions:
• Learning rate: 0.001
• Activation: ReLU
• Optimizer: Adam
• Epochs: 500
This uniform setup isolates the effect of adaptive weighting.
4.3. Pruning to leverage higher-order Chebyshev terms
We additionally investigated higher-order Chebyshev expansions 
(up to degree 6) combined with post-training pruning to reduce model 
size. Surprisingly, pruned models often outperformed their unpruned 
counterparts, demonstrating that higher-order expressivity followed by 
sparsification yields compact and accurate models.

We evaluated the Chebyshev Adaptive Network against a baseline

maintaining architectural simplicity.

5.2. When does the Chebyshev model perform best?
To understand where gains are largest, Table 1 presents the datasets 
with the largest accuracy improvements. Several trends emerge:
• High-dimensional inputs: Datasets such as movement libras (90 
features) show large gains, suggesting that adaptive weighting 
effectively captures complex interactions.
• Multi-class problems: Tasks with many classes (vowel, letter,
soybean) benefit from richer decision boundaries.
• Small and medium data: Many top-gain datasets have under 
1000 samples, indicating strong regularization and generalization 
even with limited data.
These patterns highlight the model’s strength in settings character-
ized by nonlinear structure, many features, or complex label distribu-
tions.
5.3. Error analysis
Although the Chebyshev network improves performance on most 
datasets, underperformance occurs in specific scenarios:
• Very small datasets (< 100 samples), where polynomial expan-
sion increases overfitting risk.
• Low-dimensional datasets with only a few features, limiting the 
benefit of adaptive decompositions.
• Linearly separable binary tasks, where additional expressivity 
provides no advantage.
These observations suggest that adaptivity is most useful in nonlin-
ear or moderately complex settings, while simple tasks may not justify 
additional flexibility.

5.5. Effect of polynomial order 𝑘
Increasing 𝑘 enriches representational capacity but also raises risk 
of overfitting. Fig. 8 shows that performance improves initially but 
deteriorates for large 𝑘. This behavior confirms that while the adap-
tive Chebyshev network generalizes the MLP (which corresponds to 
𝑘= 0), an improperly chosen 𝑘 can lead to overparameterization and 
poor generalization. Optimal values of 𝑘 vary by dataset, suggesting 
future work on automated selection schemes (e.g., cross-validation or 
Bayesian optimization).
5.6. Computational efficiency analysis
To assess the computational overhead introduced by adaptive
weighting, we compared training and inference times of MLPs, Atten
tion-based FCNNs, Hypernetwork-based FCNNs, and Chebyshev-
enhanced FCNNs across synthetic datasets with increasing feature 
dimensionality. As expected, MLPs remained the fastest across all 
settings, while attention and hypernetwork architectures incurred mod-
erate slowdowns. Chebyshev models exhibited the highest compu-
tational cost, with runtime increasing as both input dimensionality 
and polynomial order 𝑘 grew, reflecting the expense of polynomial 
feature expansions. Despite this overhead, the trade-off is justified by 
the substantial gains in adaptability and expressiveness demonstrated 
in our results. A full breakdown of training and inference behavior, 
including per-model scaling patterns and runtime plots, is provided in 
Appendix G.

6. Future directions
Our results demonstrate the potential of input-dependent adaptive 
weights for improving generalization and flexibility in neural networks. 
Several important directions can extend this work further.
6.1. Adaptive models for distribution shifts
Because the Chebyshev neuron adjusts weights continuously based 
on the input, it may be particularly well suited for out-of-distribution 
(OOD) or covariate-shift settings. Future work could explore how adap-
tive weighting affects robustness to dataset drift, noisy measurements, 
adversarial perturbations, or domain adaptation scenarios. Evaluating 
dynamic-weight architectures under distribution shifts remains an open 
and promising research direction.
6.2. Exploring alternative functional bases
Although Chebyshev polynomials provide a strong combination of 
stability, approximation power, and computational efficiency, other or-
thogonal bases—such as Fourier, Legendre, or Hermite polynomials—
may offer advantages for specific dataset characteristics (e.g., peri-
odic structure, heavy tails, high imbalance). A systematic comparison 
across decomposition families may reveal data-dependent criteria that 
guide optimal basis selection, forming the foundation for automated 
decomposition choice.
6.3. Multivariate adaptive weights
The current formulation uses univariate Chebyshev expansions for 
each input dimension. Extending this to multivariate polynomial ex-
pansions would allow modeling interactions directly among variables. 
Multivariate Chebyshev series provide a principled way to approxi-
mate functions 𝑓(𝑥1, … , 𝑥𝑑) through products of univariate polynomi-
als. However, full multivariate expansions scale as (𝑘+ 1)𝑑 and quickly 
become infeasible.
6.4. Scalable interaction modeling via pairwise decomposition
To manage the curse of dimensionality, a promising direction is to 
approximate multivariate functions using pairwise Chebyshev decom-
positions. Instead of modeling all 𝑑-way interactions, we approximate:

where each 𝑔𝑖𝑗 is represented through a 2D Chebyshev expansion. This 
reduces parameter growth from (𝑘+ 1)𝑑 to 𝑂(𝑑2𝑘2), while still cap-
turing rich variable interactions. Stacking such pairwise layers creates 
a hierarchy of interaction terms (illustrated in Appendix H), enabling 
scalable approximation of high-dimensional functions. Future work 
could develop neural architectures that integrate these pairwise expan-
sions as structured adaptive layers and train all coefficients through 
backpropagation.
6.5. Efficient high-dimensional approximation
Further efficiency gains may be obtained through:
• sparse or low-rank parameterizations of polynomial coefficients,
• learning which feature pairs require decomposition (feature selec-
tion),
• adaptive depth for hierarchical pairwise combinations,
• compressive techniques such as tensor-train or CP decomposition 
applied to Chebyshev coefficients.
Integrating these strategies would enable high-order adaptive mod-
els at scale, opening the door to expressive yet computationally tractable 
architectures for tabular, vision, and scientific datasets. A more detailed 
review of the possible future work can be found in Appendix H.
7. Conclusion
This paper introduces an adaptive neural network model utilizing 
Chebyshev polynomials to achieve input-dependent weighting, emu-
lating biological adaptability and enhancing the network’s ability to 
capture complex, non-linear patterns. Empirical evaluation on 145 
datasets from the PMLB benchmark reveals that the Chebyshev model 
outperforms traditional Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP) in 74% of cases, 
achieving a mean accuracy of 84.13% versus 80.87% for MLP, with 
the highest accuracy gain reaching 26.83%. Pruning further enhances

Appendix A. Properties of chebyshev polynomials
Chebyshev polynomials are a sequence of orthogonal polynomials 
that arise in various fields of applied mathematics, including approx-
imation theory and numerical analysis. They are particularly useful 
in scenarios that involve minimizing the maximum error between a 
function and its polynomial approximation, making them well-suited 
for tasks requiring efficient and accurate representations of complex 
functions. The key properties of Chebyshev polynomials, particularly 
those of the first kind 𝑇𝑗(𝑥), which are used in our adaptive neuron 
model (Mason and Handscomb, 2002), include the following:
A.1. Recursive definition
Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind, denoted 𝑇𝑗(𝑥), can be 
defined recursively. The first two polynomials in the sequence are:

This recursive definition (Mason and Handscomb, 2002) allows for

integration into the adaptive neuron framework without significantly

Orthogonality ensures that the polynomials represent independent

In the context of our model, the orthogonality property aids in reducing 
redundancy in the neuron’s response to varying inputs (Mason and 
Handscomb, 2002).
A.3. Extremal properties
Chebyshev polynomials are known for minimizing the maximum 
deviation from zero over the interval [−1, 1]. This extremal property 
makes them particularly effective in approximating functions with high 
accuracy while keeping the coefficients well-behaved. This feature is 
highly desirable in neural networks, as it can help prevent overfitting 
by providing smooth, well-distributed weights across inputs (Mason 
and Handscomb, 2002; Trefethen, 2019; Rivlin, 1990).

These roots are distributed symmetrically in the interval [−1, 1]

polynomials provide a natural way to discretize and sample the input 
space, allowing for dynamic adjustment of weights based on input 
intensities (Mason and Handscomb, 2002; Trefethen, 2019; Rivlin,

A.5. Rapid growth outside of [−1, 1]
While Chebyshev polynomials are well-behaved within the interval 
[−1, 1], their values grow rapidly for inputs outside this interval. This 
behavior can be controlled within our model by normalizing the inputs 
or using a transformation that ensures the input remains in the range 
where the polynomials are stable. This is critical in ensuring that 
the adaptive weights do not become overly sensitive to out-of-range 
inputs, thereby preserving the stability and robustness of the neuron’s 
output (Boyd, 2001; Mason and Handscomb, 2002; Trefethen, 2019; 
Rivlin, 1990).
A.6. Approximation power
Chebyshev polynomials are widely recognized for their superior 
approximation properties, especially when compared to other polyno-
mials such as Legendre or Laguerre. The series expansion in terms 
of Chebyshev polynomials is often used in Chebyshev approximation 
to achieve a near-optimal polynomial approximation of continuous 
functions. In our model, this approximation power translates into the 
ability to represent complex, non-linear relationships between the in-
put and the neuron’s response, enabling more nuanced and flexible 
learning (Mason and Handscomb, 2002; Trefethen, 2019; Rivlin, 1990).

across synthetic datasets of increasing feature dimensionality (4, 6, 9, 
11, 16, 20, 35, and 90). Each result represents the average runtime for

separately for training (forward + backward + update) and inference 
(forward only).
G.1. Training time
MLPs were consistently the most efficient, requiring only  0.00016–
0.00023 s per batch across all feature sizes. As seen in Fig. G.20 at 90

G.2. Inference time
A similar trend was observed for inference as seen in Fig. G.21. 
MLPs completed forward passes in  0.000015–0.000019 s. At 90 fea-

These results highlight clear efficiency trade-offs.
• MLPs provide unmatched speed but no dynamic input-dependent

• Attention and Hypernetworks introduce modest overheads while

• Chebyshev-based networks incur substantially higher computa-
tional cost, particularly at larger orders, due to repeated polyno-
mial expansions and feature interactions.
Nevertheless, these figures highlight the trade-off between the com-
putational efficiency of MLPs and the expressive adaptability of
Chebyshev-based neurons, justifying their increased runtime when 
richer input-conditioned representations are desired.
